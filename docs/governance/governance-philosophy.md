# Governance Philosophy

AI governance in county government is not an abstract compliance exercise. It is an operational responsibility tied to public services, civil rights, safety, privacy, legal exposure, and public trust.

## Core Belief

The platform governs public-sector AI risk. It should make evidence, findings, review status, and decisions visible.

## Governance Goals

- Know what AI systems exist.
- Know who owns each system.
- Know whether a system affects rights, safety, benefits, employment, law enforcement, or access to services.
- Know what data is used.
- Know what evidence supports deployment approval.
- Know what findings remain unresolved.
- Know what exceptions have been accepted.
- Know when a system must be blocked.

## Governance Records

Every governed AI system should have:

- Business purpose.
- Department owner.
- Technical owner.
- Public-facing or internal classification.
- Rights-impacting flag.
- Safety-impacting flag.
- Data sensitivity classification.
- Model provider and version.
- Tool/API/MCP server dependencies.
- Human review process.
- Risk tier.
- Approval status.
- Review history.
- Evidence artifacts.
- Findings history.
- Exceptions and risk acceptances.

## Governance Decisions

Decisions should be explicit:

- Approved.
- Approved with exception.
- Blocked.
- Needs security review.
- Needs bias/civil-rights review.
- Needs privacy review.
- Needs legal review.

Each decision should preserve reviewer, review date, rationale, evidence references, conditions, and exception expiration when applicable.

## Public-Sector Emphasis

County AI governance must pay special attention to:

- Language access.
- Disability access.
- Protected class impact.
- Socioeconomic proxy risk.
- Human appeal paths.
- Public benefits eligibility workflows.
- Law enforcement summaries.
- Emergency services.
- Housing and permitting.
- Employment screening.
